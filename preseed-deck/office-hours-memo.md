# Design: DORA Pre-Seed Narrative

Generated by /office-hours-style review on 2026-04-22  
Status: DRAFT  
Mode: Startup

## Problem Statement

DORA Research, Inc. is pitching an **AIOps-first iGaming operator building a prediction markets platform**. The current deck tries to sell two ideas at once:

1. a new prediction-market operator for emerging markets
2. an AI-native operating model that can run materially leaner than traditional operators

That combination is interesting, but the current deck is still closer to a **vision deck** than a **conviction deck**. It explains what DORA wants to build, but it does not yet prove why this team is uniquely right, why the first customer will move now, or what the smallest paid wedge is.

## Demand Evidence

What the deck currently proves:

- The company is at **MVP stage nearing pilot**
- There is a planned **pilot in Southeast Asia**
- The founder has a clear thesis about AI Ops reducing operator OPEX and automating the user journey

What the deck does **not** yet prove:

- That a specific operator would be **genuinely upset** if DORA disappeared tomorrow
- That a pilot customer has already committed budget, timeline, or integration effort
- That the `50%+` OPEX claim is anything more than a design target

Office-hours judgment:

- This is **promising but pre-demand**.
- “Pilot in Southeast Asia” is meaningful only if it becomes concrete: named design partner, signed pilot, paid pilot, dated launch window, or integration already underway.

## Status Quo

The strongest unspoken part of the deck is the real status quo:

- manual support teams
- fragmented CRM workflows
- market operations spread across too many people and tools
- expensive region-by-region launch overhead

That is a real problem if you can make it painfully specific.

Right now the deck names the pain in abstract terms, but not in **lived operating detail**.

The status quo slide should answer:

- What does the current operator workflow actually look like?
- How many people touch onboarding, support, CRM, and market ops?
- What are operators doing in spreadsheets, Slack, Zendesk, WhatsApp, or custom tools today?
- What does that workaround cost in dollars, launch delays, or missed retention?

If you cannot describe the workaround clearly, you do not yet own the problem.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

This is the biggest strategic gap in the deck.

The current story targets:

- “emerging markets”
- “iGaming operators”
- “prediction markets platform”

That is too broad for pre-seed.

The deck needs one painfully specific buyer/user:

- Head of Operations at a mid-market iGaming operator in Southeast Asia
- COO launching a prediction-market product line without wanting to hire a large support/ops team
- Operator founder trying to improve retention and support leverage before expanding markets

### Current narrowest wedge

The deck currently implies the wedge is:

- full-stack prediction-market operator platform

That is too big.

### Better wedge candidates

#### Wedge A: AI Ops layer for operators launching prediction products

Sell the immediate value first:

- support automation
- lifecycle/CRM orchestration
- lower operator staffing load

Then expand into the full operator platform.

#### Wedge B: Managed pilot stack for one Southeast Asia operator

Sell the first pilot as:

- “launch prediction markets without building a large ops team”

This is stronger than “we are building a whole new operator category from day one.”

#### Wedge C: Full-stack operator from day one

This is the current deck framing.
It is the most ambitious and the weakest at pre-seed unless you already have a committed pilot or unusually strong founder-market fit proof.

## Constraints

- Pre-seed round: `$1M` SAFE at `$10M` pre
- MVP, not live scale
- Deck intentionally keeps regulatory detail light
- Narrative leans into AI Ops and operator leverage
- Geography sequence is SE Asia first, then Africa, LATAM, Eastern Europe

These constraints mean the story must be:

- narrow enough to feel executable
- ambitious enough to justify venture backing
- evidence-led enough to survive partner questions

## Premises

These are the load-bearing premises in the current deck.

1. **Prediction markets are the right product wedge for DORA, not just a technically interesting format.**
2. **AI Ops is a meaningful differentiator in operator economics, not a cosmetic add-on.**
3. **A smaller team with strong agent orchestration can compete with larger legacy operator teams.**
4. **A Southeast Asia pilot can become a reusable launch playbook for Africa, LATAM, and Eastern Europe.**
5. **The market wants a leaner operator model badly enough to switch behavior, not just compliment the concept.**

My current position:

- I buy `2`, `3`, and `4` as hypotheses worth funding.
- I think `1` and `5` are still under-proven in the deck.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Sell the full vision

Summary:
Pitch DORA as the AI-native prediction-market operator for emerging markets, with the full-stack platform and global expansion path front and center.

Pros:

- Biggest story
- Most venture-scale ambition
- Makes the company feel category-defining

Cons:

- Reads broad before it reads inevitable
- Raises the proof bar dramatically
- Invites “why you, why now, why not just be software?” questions

### Approach B: Pilot-first operator wedge

Summary:
Pitch DORA as the fastest way for one operator in Southeast Asia to launch prediction markets with a lean, agent-driven ops stack.

Pros:

- Concrete
- Easier to believe at pre-seed
- Creates a clean bridge from pilot to platform

Cons:

- Slightly less grand on first impression
- Requires discipline not to re-bloat the narrative

### Approach C: AI Ops first, prediction platform second

Summary:
Pitch DORA primarily as an AI-native operating system for iGaming operators, with prediction markets as the first high-value deployment surface.

Pros:

- Sharpest economic story
- Strongest immediate “why now”
- Makes the `50%+` OPEX thesis central

Cons:

- Risks sounding like a tooling company instead of an operator
- Could weaken the uniqueness of the prediction-market story if not handled carefully

## Recommended Approach

Choose **Approach B: Pilot-first operator wedge**.

Why:

- It keeps the ambition, but gives investors one believable first hill to climb.
- It lets the deck say: *“We know exactly who the first buyer is, exactly what pain we remove, and exactly how the pilot becomes a repeatable launch playbook.”*
- It still leaves room to end on the bigger vision: DORA becomes the AI-native operator model for prediction markets.

The deck should therefore shift from:

- “We are building a huge full-stack platform for emerging markets”

to:

- “We are proving a new operator model in one pilot market, where AI Ops gives us a structural advantage from day one.”

## Open Questions

These are the questions a YC-style office hours partner would keep pressing:

1. Who is the **specific first customer**?
2. What is the **single workflow** they would pay for this week?
3. What does their current workaround cost in people, time, or delay?
4. What has John observed from inside iGaming operations that outsiders consistently miss?
5. Why is prediction markets the right first surface, instead of sportsbook ops automation or CRM tooling alone?
6. What is the concrete pilot signal you expect in the next 90 days: LOI, signed pilot, paid pilot, launch date, or usage KPI?

## Success Criteria

The next version of the deck should make an investor believe all three of these:

1. **The pain is real.**
   Not “interesting,” not “big market,” but painful enough to change buying behavior.

2. **The wedge is narrow.**
   One first user, one first buyer, one first workflow, one first pilot.

3. **The expansion path is real.**
   The Southeast Asia pilot is not a local story; it is the first repeatable operating playbook.

## The Assignment

Before revising the deck again, answer these in one page:

1. Who is the exact first buyer?
2. What exact workflow are they paying to remove?
3. What does that workflow cost them today?
4. What is the smallest version DORA can charge for during the pilot?
5. What is the strongest proof point you can honestly put on one slide by the next investor meeting?

If you can answer those cleanly, the deck gets much stronger very quickly.
